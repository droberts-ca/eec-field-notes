Field Note 1: Complex Neurodevelopmental Profile, Label Tension

Date: November 4, 2025 | School Psychologist, [Site Name Redacted]

Context

Initial eligibility determination â€” Kindergarten student with complex neurodevelopmental profile (CAS, global delays, atypical sensory-motor profile); parent declined autism eligibility consideration.

Summary

A site psychologist independently applied the EEC framework to guide eligibility reasoning for a Kindergarten student presenting with:

  • Childhood apraxia of speech (CAS)
  • Global language and cognitive delays
  • Atypical sensory-motor behaviors and physical features

The student showed intact joint attention and social reciprocity in highly structured, adult-facilitated contexts, suggesting that social success was heavily environment-mediated. Traditional categorical reasoning could have forced a debate over "which label fits best." EEC shifted the discussion toward functional access and environmental contingency.

Student Profile (Summary Data)

Domain Findings
Cognition Below-average range; relative weaknesses in verbal comprehension, visual-spatial reasoning, and nonverbal reasoning.
Language Significant receptive and expressive delays commensurate with cognitive level.
Speech Childhood apraxia of speech with inconsistent sound production and difficulty with volitional sequencing.
Motor-Sensory Atypical body movements, visual stimming, sensory-seeking behaviors.
Physical Features High-arched palate, thin upper lip, tapered fingertips (narrow distally).
Social Communication Relative strength in joint attention, social interest, and reciprocity within structured, familiar contexts.
Background Limited preschool experience; home-based care prior to Kindergarten entry.

Traditional Eligibility Challenges

  • CAS does not account for global cognitive delays.
  • Cognitive delays do not fully explain atypical sensory-motor features and physical characteristics.
  • Autistic traits are present but do not clearly meet full criteria for autism eligibility.
  • Social strengths appear highly scaffolded and adult-dependent, raising questions about generalization.
  • Risk: Team debate could fixate on label validity ("What fits best?") instead of functional access ("What supports are needed to participate in typical Kindergarten learning environments?").

EEC Reframe

Core Questions

  • In which contexts does the student demonstrate adaptive functioning and participation, and in which does she not?
  • Which environmental variables predict success vs. struggle?
  • Is the observed profile categorical (consistent across contexts) or context-dependent (performance varies with environmental scaffolds)?

EEC allowed the team to make a present-tense, access-based decision (SLI) while explicitly documenting the complexity and uncertainty of the broader neurodevelopmental picture.

EEC Documentation (Protective Language)

"From an ecological and context-dependent perspective, the student's developmental profile reflects an interaction between experiential factors (limited preschool exposure) and intrinsic neurodevelopmental differences. While structured environments currently support social and communication success, these strengths may not generalize to less scaffolded contexts. Observed features (CAS, cognitive delay, atypical sensory-motor behaviors, and physical characteristics) suggest possible underlying medical or genetic factors warranting further exploration. Current eligibility under Speech or Language Impairment provides access to necessary supports while allowing flexibility for future diagnostic clarification."

Key EEC Principles Demonstrated

  • Acknowledges complexity without over- or under-identifying.
  • Documents environmental contingency for social communication success.
  • Establishes a scaffolded baseline, not a fixed "trait profile."
  • Protects future teams with clear re-evaluation triggers and rationales.
  • Builds continuity between present need (SLI) and future developmental trajectory without locking the student into a premature category.
"EEC let me document the complexity without forcing a categorical label. The student gets services now, the parent feels respected, and the record already anticipates what might surface later."

Field Note 2: "Average" Scores, Context-Dependent Communication

Date: October 2025 | ECAT Team (School Psychologist, SLP, Special Education Teacher, Nurse)

Context

Initial multidisciplinary evaluation â€” 3-year-old preschool student with bilingual exposure (English/Spanish), outside autism diagnosis, and confirmed 1q21.1 microduplication. Standardized scores in the low-average range, but participation limited by context-dependent communication.

Summary

A preschool student with:

  • Medical/genetic diagnosis of 1q21.1 microduplication
  • Outside diagnosis of autism with accompanying language impairment
  • Bilingual exposure (English/Spanish)

presented with low-average language and adaptive scores on standardized measures. However, ecological data (home, clinic, and classroom observations) revealed:

  • Reduced verbal initiation, especially with peers
  • Brief utterances and reliance on gestures
  • Strong dependence on adult modeling, visual supports, and predictable routines for successful participation

Under a traditional, score-focused lens, the team might have concluded that the student did not qualify for services because "scores are in the average range." EEC reframed the case by asking whether her current communication allowed functional access to age-typical participation across environments.

Student Profile (Summary Data)

Domain Findings
Cognition Borderline range on DAYC-2 (Cognitive SS = 77); strengths in visual matching, puzzles, and routine-based learning.
Language DAYC-2 Communication SS = 83 (Receptive 81; Expressive 85). Uses short phrases; relies heavily on gestures and context.
Speech Age-appropriate articulation and phonology for a multilingual learner; intelligible to familiar listeners in context.
Motor-Sensory Periodic toe-walking; high sensory interest in hands-on play; benefits from sensory-rich, predictable activities.
Social Communication Consistent eye contact, joint attention, and shared enjoyment with familiar adults; limited spontaneous peer initiation.
Adaptive / Socialization Low-average adaptive skills; follows routines with support; needs prompts to manage transitions and complex directions.
Medical / Genetic 1q21.1 microduplication; outside diagnosis of Autism; no current acute or chronic health concerns impacting classroom participation.

Traditional Eligibility Challenge

  • Standardized language and adaptive scores fall in the low-average range.
  • Autism features are subtle and inconsistently observed across tools (ADOS, CARS-2, clinical history).
  • Strong nonverbal reciprocity and pretend play in structured assessment contexts.
  • Risk: team may document "average scores, mild concerns" and decline eligibility, overlooking the fact that communication is only functional under specific, high-support conditions.

EEC Reframe

Core Questions

  • In which environments does this student participate as a full communication partner, and when does she withdraw or rely on gesture-only communication?
  • Which environmental variables (structure, adult proximity, visual cues, group size, linguistic complexity) determine whether she can access learning opportunities available to peers?
  • Are her "average" skills robust across contexts or conditional on scaffolding that is not guaranteed in typical preschool routines?

Eligibility Decision

Primary Eligibility: Speech or Language Impairment (SLI), secondary to a functional communication delay.

Autism: Outside diagnosis and genetic findings acknowledged; CARS-2 results in the "Minimal to No Signs of Autism" range during this evaluation.

EEC Rationale: While standardized measures place language skills in the low-average range, ecological data show that communication is not yet sufficient to support independent participation in preschool communication demands (peer interaction, group tasks, and less scaffolded language).

EEC Documentation (Protective Language)

"From an ecological and context-dependent perspective, the student's communicative and social abilities are strongly mediated by environmental structure and adult support. Although standardized scores fall within the low-average range, observational data demonstrate that participation and verbal initiation decrease significantly when scaffolds are reduced. These findings indicate that specialized instruction is required to generalize communication and participation skills across settings, consistent with IDEA's focus on access to age-typical learning opportunities."

Key EEC Principles Demonstrated

  • Shows that "average" scores can still mask access barriers when skills are context-bound.
  • Reframes the question from "Is she delayed enough?" to "Can she access typical routines without specialized support?"
  • Anchors eligibility to participation across environments, not percentile ranks.
  • Uses non-deficit, protective language that respects outside diagnoses while focusing on current educational impact.
  • Embeds a clear rationale for SDI: environmental scaffolds are not incidental supports; they are the specialized instruction that enables access.
